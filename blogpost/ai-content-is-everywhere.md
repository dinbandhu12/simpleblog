# AI Content Is Everywhere - And It's Starting to Feel Suffocating

I opened Instagram this morning. Scrolled for maybe three minutes.

A hyper-realistic image of a sunset that never happened. A video of a celebrity saying something they never said. An "artist" dropping fifty AI-generated illustrations with a "hard work pays off 🙏" caption. A motivational reel with a perfectly cloned voice reading words no human actually wrote.

Three minutes. That's all it took.

And the worst part? Most people scrolling past it have no idea what's real anymore.

## We Are Drowning in AI Content

Let me say this plainly: **AI-generated content has completely flooded social media in 2026.** Instagram, Facebook, YouTube Shorts, X, LinkedIn it doesn't matter where you go. The feed is packed with it.

Images generated by Midjourney and DALL-E. Videos stitched together by Sora and Runway. Voiceovers cloned from real creators. Blog posts written in seconds by ChatGPT. Thumbnails, captions, entire accounts built and run by AI with little to no human touch.

And the volume is staggering. What used to take a content creator a week to produce a handful of quality posts, some thought put into each one an AI pipeline can now produce in an afternoon. Hundreds of posts. Thousands, if you push it.

The internet is filling up faster than any of us can process it. And most of it is... empty.

## Why Are Creators Doing This?

Look, I'm not here to shame anyone. The pressure creators face is real.

Social media rewards volume. The algorithm doesn't care if you poured your heart into something or if you hit generate and walked away for a coffee. Engagement is engagement. Views are views. And in a world where attention equals income, people are making a very rational if depressing choice.

AI tools let them:
- Post ten times a day without burning out
- Generate content in niches they know nothing about
- Produce "original" artwork without any artistic training
- Clone viral formats endlessly with different skins on them
And it works. The numbers go up. The followers come. The money follows.

So more creators adopt it. Then more. Then it becomes the norm. And suddenly the creator who is actually sitting down, actually thinking, actually making something from scratch they're competing against a machine that never sleeps, never gets tired, and never runs out of ideas to steal.

That's the game right now. And it's a rigged one.

## The Real Cost of All This

Here's where it gets heavier. Because this isn't just about aesthetics or "the feed not feeling the same anymore." The consequences are actually serious.

### Creativity is quietly dying

There's a certain kind of creative work that only happens when a human sits with discomfort long enough to make something honest. A painting born from grief. A photograph taken at exactly the right moment because someone was actually there. A piece of writing that only exists because one specific person lived through one specific thing.

AI cannot do that. It can imitate the surface of it, convincingly. But imitation is not creation.

When AI content floods every platform, it doesn't just take up space it sets a new baseline. Audiences get trained to expect the polished, the perfect, the algorithmic. Real human work, with its rough edges and genuine emotion, starts to look *worse* by comparison. Not because it is worse, but because we've recalibrated what "good" looks like.

That is a slow, quiet loss that we're not talking about enough.

### Fake news has a new best friend

This one is urgent.

AI image and video generation has made misinformation cheaper and easier to produce than at any point in human history. You don't need a team. You don't need a budget. You need a prompt and ten seconds.

Fabricated images of disasters that didn't happen. Deepfake videos of politicians making statements they never made. Fake "eyewitness" footage of events, generated and posted before any real journalists even arrive on the scene.

By the time the truth catches up if it ever does the fake has already been shared a hundred thousand times. The damage is already done. People already believe it. And in some cases, they act on it.

This is not a future problem. This is happening right now.

### Real people are losing real livelihoods
![Person scrolling social media at night with AI-generated posts in the feed](/assets/bpimgs/b7-2.png|Late night phone scrolling as algorithmic and AI-made images and videos fill the social feed relevant to misinformation and creative displacement.)

We already watched AI coding tools like Cursor, ChatGPT, and Claude quietly start doing work that used to belong to junior developers. That conversation is still raw for a lot of people in tech.

But now the same disruption is hitting a completely different set of people.

The graphic designer who spent years building a style. The illustrator who finally had a sustainable freelance income. The video editor. The photographer. The voiceover artist. The copywriter.

These are not people who work in Silicon Valley. These are often independent, self-employed, creative people who built something real a skill, a reputation, a client base and are now watching AI undercut them at every angle.

Companies that used to hire a designer for a campaign now prompt one instead. Stock photo platforms are being buried under millions of AI images. Creative agencies are quietly shrinking their teams.

It's not hypothetical. The layoffs are happening. The contracts are drying up. And unlike the tech world, which at least has a discourse around this, the creative industry is largely absorbing the blow in silence.

## The Advantages - Because I'll Be Fair

I'm not going to pretend there's no upside. There is.

AI content tools have made creative expression accessible to people who previously had no entry point. Someone with a vision but no technical skill can now bring it to life. Small businesses that couldn't afford designers can now produce decent visuals. Indie filmmakers are experimenting with tools that would've cost millions five years ago.

That democratization is real and genuinely good.

AI also handles the tedious. Resizing assets. Writing first drafts. Generating variations. There's real value in letting machines do the mechanical, repetitive parts of creative work so humans can focus on the meaningful decisions.

Used *thoughtfully*, as a tool in a human's hands, AI can enhance creativity rather than replace it.

The keyword, though, is thoughtfully. And right now, thoughtful is not what's happening at scale on social media.

## So Why Should This Stop - Or At Least Slow Down?

Because we are building habits, and habits become culture.

If the next generation grows up in a feed where almost nothing is real where images are generated, voices are cloned, emotions are simulated what does that do to how they perceive authenticity? What does it do to empathy? To the ability to tell truth from fiction?

We already have a trust problem on the internet. AI content is pouring fuel on it.

And beyond the philosophical there are people behind every creative profession. Storytellers, artists, musicians, photographers. Real humans who see the world in particular ways and have spent their lives learning how to show it to others. That matters. Culturally, socially, it matters.

If we let the algorithm decide that generated content is "good enough," we will get a world that is optimized and empty. Technically perfect and completely hollow.

## What We Can Actually Do

This isn't hopeless. But it does require choices small, deliberate ones.

**Be conscious about what you consume.** Learn to spot AI content. Not to shame it, but to be aware. Seek out human-made work intentionally.

**Support real creators.** If someone's work moves you, tell them. Share it. Subscribe. Pay for it if you can. Human creativity needs to be economically viable or it won't survive.

**Push platforms for transparency.** AI-generated content should be labeled. Clearly, consistently, always. This is not a radical ask. It's basic honesty.

**Don't outsource your voice.** If you're a creator a writer, a designer, a photographer resist the temptation to replace your perspective with a generated one just to keep up with volume. Your specific, particular, human point of view is the thing that cannot be replicated. Protect it.

## A Closing Thought

The problem isn't that AI can create things. That's remarkable, honestly. The problem is what happens when creation stops being about expression and starts being purely about output.

When quantity kills quality. When convenience kills craft. When the feed becomes a wall of generated noise that nobody really made and nobody really feels.

I still believe in the internet as a place where humans connect with other humans. Where someone makes something, puts it out into the world, and another person somewhere is genuinely affected by it.

That's worth protecting.

Even if the algorithm doesn't reward it.
