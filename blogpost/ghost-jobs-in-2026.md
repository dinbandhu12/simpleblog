# Ghost Jobs in 2026 - I Applied to 500+ Jobs and Here's What Nobody Tells You

Three months. 500+ applications. And you know how many genuine responses I got?

Not even close to what I deserved. Not even close to what anyone applying honestly deserves.

I'm an engineering graduate. I can code. I know my stuff. And yet, here I am - refreshing my inbox every morning, watching the number of "no response" applications climb like a scoreboard nobody asked to play. Some days I question everything. Some days I still have that stubborn little voice in my head that says *keep going, something will come*.

But recently, I stopped blaming myself and started researching. And what I found made me angry. Not sad. **Angry.**

Because the system is broken. And the companies know it.

## What Is a Ghost Job?

A ghost job is a job posting that looks completely real - company logo, job description, apply button and everything - but the position either doesn't exist, was already filled internally, or was never going to be filled at all.

It's not a scam website. It's not a fake company. It's a **real company, on a real job board, with a real posting that is a complete lie.**

And it is happening on an absolutely massive scale.

## The Numbers That Should Make You Furious

Let me put some facts on the table, because when I found these I had to put my phone down for a minute.

According to a 2025 study by Greenhouse, a major hiring platform, between 18% and 22% of all online job postings are ghost jobs roughly one in five job listings you see is completely fake.

But it gets worse.

A LiveCareer survey of 918 HR professionals in March 2025 revealed that 45% of HR professionals admit they "regularly" post ghost jobs. Another 48% say they post them "occasionally." Combined, that means 93% of HR professionals engage in this practice to some degree. Only 2% said they never post ghost jobs.

Read that again. **Only 2% of HR professionals never post ghost jobs.**

In an analysis of June 2025 Bureau of Labor Statistics data, employers reported having 7.4 million job openings, but made only 5.2 million hires. Nearly one in three job postings never resulted in a hire.

Around 2020, roughly eight out of ten jobs posted online actually ended up with someone being hired. Today, that number has dropped to roughly four out of ten.

So when I'm sending out applications and hearing nothing back it's not just me. It's not my resume. It's not my skills. Half the jobs I'm applying to **may not actually exist.**

## Why Do Companies Do This?

This is the part that really got me. Because they have reasons. Calculated, deliberate, cynical reasons.

### 1. Building a Talent Pipeline (At Your Expense)
Companies post jobs to collect resumes for potential future hiring, essentially treating job seekers as a free database they can access when convenient. There's no job - just reconnaissance. Your resume, your effort, your tailored cover letter - it goes into their system and sits there until they decide to bother.

### 2. Looking Good to Investors and the Public
A company with 200 open positions looks like it is scaling fast. That perception matters for fundraising rounds, stock price, and competitive positioning. Some companies keep listings live purely for optics.

In other words - they're using fake job postings to inflate how impressive they look. Your hope is their PR strategy.

### 3. Keeping Current Employees Scared
According to survey data, 77% of managers who post ghost jobs say it increases productivity among current employees. When your team sees the company "hiring for your role," the implicit message is clear: you are replaceable.

This one might be the most ruthless thing I've ever read about corporate behavior.

### 4. Testing the Market for Salary Data
Specialized positions in IT require unique skills. Companies post ghost jobs to constantly monitor the availability of such specialists and their salary expectations. They're using your application to figure out what they'd have to pay someone with zero intention of paying anyone right now.

### 5. Internal Candidates Were Always Going to Get It
Some organizations post positions they intend to fill internally, either to comply with fair hiring requirements or to see if someone unexpectedly impressive applies. The internal candidate gets the job regardless, making external applications essentially decorative.

## The India Reality - This Hits Different Here

Now let me talk about what's happening closer to home, because the global numbers are bad, but the India-specific situation for people like me engineering graduates trying to get into IT is something else entirely.

Among engineering students at the Indian Institute of Information Technology, Design and Manufacturing, fewer than 25% secured job offers as of early 2026. "It is really bad out there," one student told Rest of World. "Everyone is so panicked even our juniors."

In early 2026, the Indian IT sector has seen over 55,000 job cuts globally, impacting freshers most severely. Wipro has reduced its fresher hiring guidance to 7,500–8,000, down from earlier estimates of 10,000. Deferred joining dates of 7+ months are being reported by hundreds of recruits.

India's urban unemployment rate rose to 7.1%, while the youth unemployment rate in urban areas, among those aged 15 to 29 years, spiked to nearly 19%.

And here is the dynamic that really nobody is talking about clearly:

TCS cut 12,000 employees in 2025 as it pivoted to becoming an AI-first services company. Those are experienced professionals - 4 years, 6 years, 8 years of experience - suddenly in the job market. And where are they applying? Entry-level roles. Because they just need something.

So freshers aren't competing with other freshers anymore. They're competing with senior developers who are desperate enough to apply downward. The competition has completely changed, and nobody adjusted the expectations.

## About Accenture - And Companies Like Them

I applied to Accenture. I think most people reading this have. And I think most people reading this have also heard... absolutely nothing back.

Accenture reduced its workforce by around 11,000 employees in 2025, citing changes in client demand including increased interest in generative AI solutions, which affected staffing requirements in traditional consulting areas.

Accenture has also warned that entry-level roles in testing, support, and basic programming will be affected first as enterprise AI deployments scale.

So let me get this straight - Accenture is cutting thousands of people, warning that entry-level roles are going away because of AI, and simultaneously still posting entry-level openings on job boards that candidates apply to and never hear back from.

That is the ghost job cycle in its purest form. And Accenture is far from the only company doing it.

## The ATS Wall - The First Rejection You Never See

Even if a job is real, you have to get past the robot first.

Before a human ever reads your application, an Applicant Tracking System (ATS) filters it. It scans for keywords, formats, specific phrases. If your resume isn't formatted exactly right, or if you're missing a specific buzzword you're out. Not rejected by a person. Rejected by an algorithm before any human ever saw your name.

With AI-assisted screening tools layered on top of ATS, vague project descriptions are being downranked. The number even a small, honest one is what the screening model extracts.

So you spend an hour tailoring your resume. You write a thoughtful cover letter. You hit submit. And within seconds, a machine decides you don't exist for a job that also might not exist. It's a double layer of fiction that costs real people real months of their lives.

## What the Government Is (Not) Doing

Some countries are at least trying to address this.

California passed legislation in March 2025, requiring all private employers who publicly advertise job postings to include a clear statement disclosing whether the posting is for a vacancy. New Jersey introduced identical bills. Ontario, Canada enacted a law in 2026 requiring employers to disclose whether a job posting represents a current vacancy.

At the federal level in the US, the proposed Truth in Job Advertising and Accountability Act would require employers to disclose intent to hire, salary bands, and deadlines for removing stale posts.

And in India? Nothing. Not a single piece of legislation targeting ghost job postings. No requirement to disclose whether a role is actually open. No penalty for leaving a filled position listed for months while thousands of people waste their time applying.

The government that should be protecting young job seekers especially in a country producing millions of engineering graduates every year into a market that can't absorb them is completely silent on this specific issue. While youth unemployment among 15–29 year olds sits near 19% in urban areas, there is no framework whatsoever to hold companies accountable for fake job postings.

That silence is a policy choice. And it has consequences.

## What You Can Actually Do Right Now

I'm still figuring this out myself. But here's what I've learned the hard way:

**Track everything.** If a job has been posted for more than 30 days and you haven't heard anything assume it's a ghost. Redirect your energy.

**Go direct, not just through portals.** LinkedIn Easy Apply is where ghost jobs thrive. Find the actual hiring manager. Send a cold message. It feels uncomfortable but it bypasses the ATS wall entirely.

**Look for verified listings.** LinkedIn reports that more than half its listings are now tagged as "verified," indicating confirmed open positions. Filter for those when possible.

**Spot the red flags.** Job reposted every 2–3 weeks with the same description? Ghost. Requirements that are wildly mismatched (entry-level role asking for 5+ years experience)? Ghost or designed to never be filled. No salary range, vague team description, generic everything? Proceed with caution.

**Don't internalize the silence.** This is the one I struggle with most. When you apply to 500 jobs and 400 don't respond that is not a reflection of your worth. It's a reflection of a broken system. The rejection (or the silence) is not about you. It literally may not be about anything to do with you at all.

I'm writing this at 11pm after another day of refreshing my inbox.

I'm frustrated. I'm tired. Some days I question whether any of this is real whether the job market I was promised when I started my engineering degree ever actually existed the way they described it.

But I also know this: the more I understand how the system works t  he ghost postings, the ATS filters, the overexperienced candidates flooding entry-level applications, the companies collecting resumes with zero intention of calling the less I blame myself for the silence.

The system is rigged. Not against me specifically. Against everyone who applies honestly, with real effort, into a machine designed to collect that effort without giving anything back.

And I think it's worth saying that out loud. Clearly. Without dressing it up.

Because if more of us talk about this honestly as job seekers, as graduates, as people who did everything "right" maybe the conversation starts to shift. Maybe companies start to feel accountable. Maybe the government wakes up.

Until then keep applying. Stay sharp. And don't let a ghost make you feel like you're the one who doesn't exist.
